Decision Engine

How Questions Get Answered

When an employee asks a question on Slack, the bot doesn't keyword-search through documents. It reasons through the authority chain, fetching only what it needs.

1

Employee Asks a Question

Via DM or @mention on Slack. Example: "A partner in Indore is approaching customers directly and offering them personal deals. What do we do?"

2

Identify the OS Domain

The bot classifies: which of the 20 Operating Systems does this fall under? → Exit OS (L5.17) + Enforcement & FPV OS (L5.12).

3

Check: Does a Formal OS Exist?

The bot checks whether an L5.* document has been drafted for this domain.

Yes — OS exists (e.g., L5.17 Exit OS)
Fetch the specific document via lookup_document tool. Answer directly from it, citing sections, state IDs, lever IDs, and playbooks.
No — OS not yet drafted (e.g., L5.12 Enforcement)
Derive guidance by traversing the authority chain top-down: primitives (L3.5) → guards (L3.4) → cluster rules (L4.2) → OSTF template (L4.3). Clearly flag the answer as derived.
4

Generate Response (Max 20 Lines)

Lead with the answer (5–7 lines), then list immediate actions (5–8 bullets). Offer "reply 'details' for full breakdown." Concise, actionable, Slack-native.

5

Check Feedback Triggers

The bot evaluates 5 trigger conditions. If any fire, it auto-posts structured feedback to #os-feedback on Slack.

5 Feedback Triggers
T1 Employee challenges the answer
T2 Genuine edge case — primitives don't clearly resolve
T3 Employee explicitly asks to escalate
T4 Bot's confidence is low
T5 HIGH-STAKES — B2 termination, FPV, routing freeze, access revocation (auto-flag, no prompt needed)
Design Principles

Systems Thinking — Where We Intervene

The Wiom OS architecture is designed using Donella Meadows' 12 Leverage Points framework. Instead of operating at low-leverage parameter tweaks (Level 12), we intervene at the highest leverage points: paradigms, goals, rules, and information flows.

1
The power to transcend paradigms
→ The feedback flywheel lets the OS evolve its own paradigm over time
2
The mindset or paradigm out of which the system arises
→ L1 Root Context: "Platform, not franchise. System-led, not sales-driven."
3
The goals of the system
→ L2 System Reset + L3 Primitives: Customer primacy > Partner viability > Design objectives
4
The power to self-organize system structure
→ OSTF template: any team can draft new OS docs that plug into the hierarchy
5
The rules of the system (incentives, constraints)
→ L3 Guards (F1–F4, E1–E3, S1–S3): binary constraints that cannot be overridden
6
The structure of information flows
→ #os-feedback loop: employee questions surface OS gaps → SD updates → system improves
7
The gain around driving positive feedback loops
8
Negative feedback loops relative to impacts they correct
→ Pattern-based enforcement (P6): weekly governance corrects drift before it compounds
9
The lengths of delays
10
The structure of material stocks and flows
11
The sizes of buffers
12
Constants, parameters, numbers (least leverage)

Highlighted rows = where the Wiom OS architecture actively intervenes. We operate at Levels 1–6 and 8 — the highest leverage points in the system.
